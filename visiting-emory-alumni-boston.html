Personal Update

Visiting Emory Alumni in Boston

Connecting with leaders across biotechnology, healthcare, policy, and the arts to learn how they have built meaningful careers beyond traditional boundaries.

Last month, I had the opportunity to travel to Boston and connect with Emory alumni and professionals across healthcare, biotechnology, policy, and the arts.

What stood out to me was that, despite working in very different fields, each person found ways to move beyond the traditional boundaries of their role.

Dr. Nabiha Saklayen shared the journey of founding Cellino, a biotech company developing iPSC-derived therapies for diseases such as Type 2 Diabetes and Parkinson's. What interested me was her emphasis on communication across disciplines — if an idea isn't working or needs tweaking, she wants to hear it. This means building spaces where all employees, from bioengineers to software engineers, can collaborate and speak up.

At GrubStreet, Jonathan Todd reflected on how he balanced creative passions with a more traditional career path. As a self-taught artist, he always sought opportunities to create — from distributing comics on the playground as a kid to creating political cartoons while working as a copywriter. That sustained passion not only led him to freelance with Scholastic, but to share it with others, teaching students in Boston to create their own comics.

Jessie Gaeta discussed caring for patients through Boston Health Care for the Homeless Program's unique respite program, which provides short-term shelter to homeless patients. As their former Chief Medical Officer, she realized that lasting care requires more than treating individuals — and has since worked with local representatives to advocate for housing policy reforms to address structural conditions driving poor health outcomes.

These conversations reinforced that people making a real difference don't just stay in their lane. They build collaborative teams, sustain creative pursuits alongside their careers, and tackle large problems from multiple angles. I'm excited to carry these lessons forward as I continue to explore public health, policy, and innovation at Emory.

Thank you to all the other Emory alumni and professionals I met, including Fatima Cody Stanford and Melanie Racine, and to Janet Gonzales and the Emory Scholars Program for making opportunities like this possible.

Grateful to have shared this experience with Talya Castell, Ayi Ekhaese, Ella Griswold, Kushal Maganti, Hira Malik, Esther Oyetunji, Carys Peden, Nicole Phiri, Muhammad Rizwan, Soha Sewani, Mariia Sukhomlinova, Jenny Tham, Thomas Winning, and Epherata Zeleke.
